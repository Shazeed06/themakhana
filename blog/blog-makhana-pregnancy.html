Makhana During Pregnancy: Benefits, Safety and Servings

By The Makhana · Updated 2 July 2026 · 6 min read

Yes — for most healthy pregnancies, makhana (foxnuts or phool makhana) is a safe and wholesome snack when eaten in normal food amounts. These roasted lotus seeds are naturally gluten-free, low in calories per handful and offer plant protein, fibre and minerals, which makes them an easy swap for fried or sugary munchies. As with any food during pregnancy, enjoy makhana in moderation and check with your doctor if you have allergies or specific medical concerns.

Quick answer

  • Makhana is generally safe in pregnancy when eaten in normal snack amounts.
  • It offers plant protein, fibre, calcium and magnesium in a light, crunchy form.
  • A typical serving is about 25–30 g a day (roughly one handful).
  • Choose lightly salted or plain roasted makhana over very spicy or over-salted versions.

Is makhana safe during pregnancy?

Makhana is simply the roasted seed of the lotus plant, eaten in Indian kitchens for generations. For most expecting mothers it is a gentle, easy-to-digest snack with no caffeine, no added sugar and no gluten. The key is how it is prepared: our makhana is roasted in cold-pressed or olive oil, never deep-fried, with no palm oil, maida or MSG. If you have a known allergy, a history of food sensitivities, or any condition your doctor is monitoring, mention makhana to them before making it a regular snack. This article is general information and not a substitute for medical advice.

What are the benefits of makhana in pregnancy?

During pregnancy your body works harder, and small, nutrient-dense snacks can help. Makhana brings a useful mix of nutrients in a low-calorie package:

  • Plant protein: makhana provides around 9 g of protein per 100 g, supporting the extra protein needs of pregnancy as part of a balanced diet.
  • Calcium & magnesium: foxnuts contribute these minerals, which are part of overall bone and muscle health for both mother and baby.
  • Iron-supporting snack: makhana can sit alongside iron-rich foods in your day, complementing the wider diet your doctor recommends.
  • Fibre for digestion: the fibre in makhana can support regular digestion, which many women find helpful when constipation is common in pregnancy.
  • Low-calorie craving fix: a crunchy handful satisfies the urge to snack without the grease of fried namkeen or the sugar of biscuits.

Remember that no single food does it all. Makhana is best as one part of a varied, balanced pregnancy diet rather than a replacement for meals or prescribed supplements.

How much makhana should you eat per day?

A practical everyday serving is about 25–30 g of makhana — roughly one large handful or a small bowl. That is usually enough to feel satisfied between meals without crowding out other foods. A few simple pointers:

  1. Treat makhana as a snack, not a meal replacement.
  2. Spread your eating across the day rather than finishing a big bowl in one go.
  3. Watch the seasoning — heavily salted or buttery versions add up quickly.
  4. Drink water alongside, especially because the fibre works best when you stay hydrated.

If your doctor has advised you to watch your salt, calories or specific nutrients, let that guidance set your portion. For a fuller breakdown that applies beyond pregnancy too, see our guide on how many makhana per day and the side effects of makhana and who should avoid foxnuts.

Which makhana should you choose?

Not all makhana is equal once it is seasoned. For pregnancy, lean towards the gentler options:

  • Pick lightly salted or plain roasted foxnuts, such as our Classic Lightly Salted, so you control the flavour without excess salt.
  • Go easy on very spicy or masala-heavy variants if you are prone to heartburn or acidity, which is common in later pregnancy.
  • Avoid over-salted, deep-fried or palm-oil packs — these undo much of what makes makhana a smart snack.
  • Check the label for no maida, no MSG and no palm oil, and look for roasting in cold-pressed or olive oil.

When you want a wholesome crunch, you can shop our makhana range and choose the seasoning that feels right for you.

How can you enjoy makhana while expecting?

Makhana is wonderfully flexible, so it is easy to keep things interesting:

  • Keep a small jar of lightly salted makhana handy for mid-morning or evening hunger.
  • Toss plain roasted foxnuts into a bowl of curd with fruit for a light snack.
  • Add them to a trail mix with nuts and seeds for a slow-burning energy bite.
  • Stir crushed makhana into kheer or soups for a little extra body.

A note on safety and your doctor

Every pregnancy is different. While makhana is widely enjoyed and considered a gentle food, this article shares general information, not medical advice. If you have gestational diabetes, are managing your weight on medical guidance, have food allergies, or simply want reassurance, speak with your doctor or gynaecologist before changing your diet. They know your history and can tailor advice to you and your baby.

Is makhana good for gestational diabetes?

Makhana has a relatively low glycaemic index and provides fibre and protein, so a small plain-roasted portion can fit into a diabetes-friendly snack plan. For most people makhana is easy to digest and does not cause gas. Eating a very large amount at once, or not drinking enough water with its fibre, can occasionally lead to mild bloating. Stick to a handful at a time, chew well and stay hydrated to keep digestion comfortable.
