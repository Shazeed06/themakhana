Makhana Nutrition: Calories, Protein and Full Nutritional Value

By The Makhana · Updated 22 June 2026 · 6 min read

Makhana nutrition is genuinely impressive for a snack: roughly 340-350 calories per 100g, with around 9-10g of plant protein, 65g of carbohydrate, close to 7g of fibre and almost no fat. Foxnuts are naturally gluten-free, low in sodium, and carry useful amounts of calcium, magnesium and potassium. In a realistic 30g serving, that comes to about 100-105 calories — light enough for an everyday handful.

Quick answer

  • Calories: ~340-350 per 100g of raw makhana; ~100-105 in a 30g serving.
  • Protein: ~9-10g per 100g, a useful plant-based protein source.
  • Carbs & fibre: ~65g carbohydrate and ~7g fibre per 100g.
  • Fat: very low — under 1g per 100g in raw foxnuts.
  • Minerals: naturally rich in calcium, magnesium, potassium and phosphorus, and low in sodium.

What is the full nutritional value of makhana per 100g?

Makhana (foxnuts or phool makhana) is the popped, dried seed of the lotus plant. Exact figures vary a little by batch, variety and how the seeds are roasted, but the approximate per-100g profile for raw makhana looks like this:

These numbers are typical averages for plain foxnuts. Added salt, masala or seasoning will change the sodium figure, so always check the pack if you are watching your salt intake.

How much protein does makhana have?

At roughly 9-10g of protein per 100g, makhana stands out among everyday Indian snacks. A 30g serving delivers about 3g — not enormous on its own, but a clean addition to a plant-forward diet. Makhana is not a complete protein, so it is best thought of as one contributor among many across the day, alongside pulses, dairy or nuts.

  • Plant-based: suitable for vegetarian and vegan snacking.
  • Low fat: the protein comes without the oil load of fried snacks.
  • Versatile: easy to add to a chaat, trail mix or evening bowl.

Why are makhana calories so low?

A 30g serving of plain makhana is only around 100 calories because the seed is light, airy and almost fat-free. Compare that with the same weight of fried chips or namkeen, where added oil quickly pushes calories far higher. The catch is how makhana is cooked: deep-frying or heavy palm-oil roasting adds calories you did not bargain for.

That is exactly why we slow-roast our foxnuts in cold-pressed or olive oil and never deep-fry them — with no palm oil, no maida and no MSG. You keep the naturally light calorie count and the honest crunch.

Roasted vs raw makhana: does the nutrition change?

The underlying nutrition of raw and roasted makhana is very similar — the same protein, carbs and minerals are present in both. What changes is the cooking method:

  1. Raw / plain roasted: closest to the base profile above, with minimal added fat.
  2. Lightly oil-roasted: a small rise in fat and calories, depending on the oil and quantity used.
  3. Deep-fried or palm-oil snacks: a noticeably higher fat and calorie load — the opposite of why people reach for makhana.

What minerals and micronutrients does makhana provide?

Beyond the headline macros, makhana contributes a worthwhile spread of minerals:

  • Calcium — supports bones and teeth.
  • Magnesium — involved in muscle and nerve function.
  • Potassium — an electrolyte that pairs well with a low-sodium snack.
  • Phosphorus — works alongside calcium in bone health.

Because plain makhana is naturally low in sodium, it can be a sensible swap for salty fried snacks — provided you go easy on added seasoning. What is a sensible portion size for makhana?

A practical serving is about 30g, which is a generous handful or roughly a couple of small bowls of the popped seeds. At around 100 calories, that is a satisfying between-meals snack. Makhana is filling thanks to its fibre and volume, but, like any food, calories add up if you keep refilling the bowl — so portion awareness still matters.
