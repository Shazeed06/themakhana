What is Makhana? The Complete Guide to Foxnuts (Phool Makhana)

By The Makhana · Updated 22 June 2026 · 7 min read

Makhana is the light, white, puffed snack made from the popped seeds of the prickly water-lily, Euryale ferox. You may also know it as foxnut, phool makhana, lotus seed or gorgon nut. The raw seeds are harvested, dried and roasted until they pop into airy puffs that can be enjoyed plain, spiced or roasted in oil.

Quick answer

  • Makhana = popped foxnuts, the seeds of the prickly water-lily (Euryale ferox).
  • Common names: foxnut, phool makhana, lotus seed and gorgon nut.
  • Most Indian makhana is grown and harvested in Bihar.
  • It is naturally gluten-free, non-GMO and a source of plant protein (around 9g per 100g).
  • Best enjoyed roasted, never deep-fried - as a snack, in curries or in kheer.

What exactly is makhana?

Makhana is the edible seed of the prickly water-lily, a flowering aquatic plant known botanically as Euryale ferox. The plant grows in ponds and shallow wetlands, producing seeds inside a spiky pod. Once harvested, the hard seeds are dried and then roasted at high heat until the kernel inside pops, expanding into the soft, snow-white puff we recognise as makhana.

In its popped form, makhana is light, crunchy and mild. It works beautifully on its own as a snack, and it is also a classic ingredient in Indian cooking - from rich gravies to festive kheer. Why is it called foxnut or phool makhana?

Makhana goes by several names, which can be confusing at first. Here is what each one means:

  • Foxnut - the most common English name, used widely in India and abroad.
  • Phool makhana - "phool" means flower, a nod to the water-lily it comes from.
  • Lotus seed - an everyday name, though true lotus seeds come from a related plant.
  • Gorgon nut - an older English term derived from the plant's spiky appearance.

All of these point to the same thing: the popped seeds of Euryale ferox. A quick note on the lotus comparison - the seeds sold simply as "lotus seeds" often come from the sacred lotus (Nelumbo nucifera), a different but related water plant. In Indian usage, makhana, phool makhana and foxnut are interchangeable.

How is makhana grown and harvested in Bihar?

India produces the vast majority of the world's makhana, and most of it comes from Bihar. The state's ponds and wetlands offer ideal conditions for the prickly water-lily, and generations of farming families have built deep expertise in growing and processing the crop.

The journey from pond to pouch looks roughly like this:

  1. Growing - the water-lily flowers and sets seed beneath the water's surface in ponds and low-lying fields.
  2. Harvesting - the seeds are gathered by hand, often by wading into the water, then washed and graded.
  3. Drying - the raw seeds are sun-dried to the right moisture level.
  4. Roasting and popping - the dried seeds are roasted at high heat until the kernels pop into light puffs.
  5. Cleaning and packing - the puffs are cleaned, sorted by size and packed.

Raw vs roasted makhana - what is the difference?

Both raw and roasted makhana start from the same popped seed, but they are at different stages of preparation:

  • Raw phool makhana is the plain, unseasoned puff. It is light and a little chewy, and most people give it a quick dry roast or cook it before eating. Raw makhana is the versatile base for kheer, curries and homemade roasted snacks.
  • Roasted makhana is ready to eat. It is roasted - at The Makhana, in cold-pressed or olive oil, never deep-fried - and often seasoned. The result is crisp, crunchy and flavoured, straight from the pouch.

What does makhana taste like?

On its own, makhana has a very mild, slightly nutty flavour and an airy, crisp texture - a little like a lighter popcorn or a puffed cracker. Because the base flavour is so gentle, makhana is a wonderful carrier for seasoning. A pinch of salt and pepper, a peri peri coating, ghee and spices, or even a sweet dusting can completely change its character.

This mildness is exactly why it has become a favourite for everyday snacking: it pairs well with chai, sits comfortably in a lunchbox, and never feels heavy.

How do you eat makhana?

One of the best things about makhana is how flexible it is. A few easy ways to enjoy it:

  • As a ready-to-eat snack - open a roasted pack and enjoy with chai or on the go.
  • Quick home roast - toss raw makhana in a little ghee or cold-pressed oil with salt and spices, and dry roast until crisp.
  • In curries - add to rich gravies for a soft, satisfying bite.
  • As makhana kheer - simmer in milk with cardamom and a touch of sweetness for a classic dessert.
  • In trail mixes - combine with nuts and seeds for a balanced, crunchy mix.
