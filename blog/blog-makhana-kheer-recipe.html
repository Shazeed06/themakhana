Makhana Kheer Recipe: Creamy, Vrat-Friendly Kheer in 20 Minutes

By The Makhana · Published 2 July 2026 · 6 min read

Makhana kheer is the dessert that saves every vrat menu — ghee-roasted foxnuts simmered in full-cream milk with cardamom and saffron until the puffs turn soft and the milk thickens into a creamy, festive kheer. Because makhana counts as phalahar, this kheer is a natural fit for Sawan somvar, Navratri and Ekadashi fasts, and it needs no rice, no soaking and barely 20 minutes on the stove. Whether you are cooking for a fasting day or simply want a lighter take on rice kheer, this recipe works with one cup of makhana and a litre of milk.

Quick answer

  • Roast 1 cup makhana in 1 tbsp ghee until crisp, then crush half for creaminess.
  • Simmer in 1 litre full-cream milk for 10–12 minutes until soft and thick.
  • Sweeten with sugar, or 4–5 dates for vrat; finish with cardamom, saffron and nuts.
  • Roast the makhana while the milk heats on a second burner and the whole recipe stays around 20 minutes.
  • Vrat-friendly as long as you use vrat-approved sweeteners and sendha namak only.

Why makhana kheer works so well for vrat

Traditional rice kheer is off the menu on most fasting days, but makhana, milk, ghee, dates, cardamom and saffron are all widely accepted as falahari ingredients. That makes makhana kheer one of the few desserts you can serve straight through a fast without second-guessing the ingredient list. The foxnuts also behave beautifully in milk: the whole puffs turn soft and dumpling-like, while the crushed ones dissolve and thicken the kheer naturally — no rice, no cornflour. For the full list of what is allowed on fasting days, see our guide to makhana for fasting and vrat.

Ingredients (serves 4)

  • 1 cup raw phool makhana — plain and unflavoured; our raw phool makhana is exactly this
  • 1 litre full-cream milk — full-cream gives the richest, creamiest kheer
  • 3–4 tbsp sugar, or 4–5 seedless dates if you are making it for vrat
  • 1 tbsp ghee for roasting
  • ½ tsp cardamom powder
  • 8–10 saffron strands, soaked in 2 tbsp warm milk
  • 2 tbsp chopped nuts — almonds, pistachios, cashews
  • A tiny pinch of sendha namak (optional) — if you add any salt at all on a fasting day, it must be sendha namak, not table salt

How to make makhana kheer, step by step

Use two burners if you can — roast the makhana in one pan while the milk comes to a boil in another — and the recipe stays comfortably inside 20 minutes.

  1. Roast the makhana in ghee. Heat 1 tablespoon ghee in a heavy pan on low. Add 1 cup makhana and roast for 6 to 8 minutes, stirring constantly, until the puffs are crisp and snap cleanly when pressed. Set aside to cool.
  2. Crush half the makhana. Once cool, crush half the roasted makhana coarsely with your hands or a rolling pin. Keep the rest whole. The crushed bits dissolve into the milk and make the kheer creamy.
  3. Simmer the milk. Bring 1 litre full-cream milk to a boil in a heavy-bottomed pan, then lower the heat and let it simmer, stirring occasionally so it does not catch at the bottom.
  4. Add the makhana and simmer. Add the whole and crushed makhana to the milk. Simmer on low for 10 to 12 minutes, stirring every couple of minutes, until the puffs turn soft and the kheer starts to thicken.
  5. Sweeten the kheer. Stir in 3 to 4 tablespoons sugar, or 4 to 5 chopped seedless dates if you are making it for vrat. Cook for 2 minutes until fully dissolved.
  6. Add cardamom and saffron. Stir in half a teaspoon of cardamom powder and the saffron strands along with their soaking milk. Simmer for 1 more minute so the flavours bloom.
  7. Garnish and serve. Switch off the heat and top with chopped almonds, pistachios and cashews. Serve warm, or chill for 2 to 3 hours for a thicker, colder kheer.

Pro tips for the creamiest makhana kheer

  • Crush for creaminess. The half-crushed makhana is what thickens the milk. Want a thicker, halwai-style kheer? Crush two-thirds instead of half.
  • Roast low and slow. Under-roasted makhana turns chewy in milk instead of soft. If your puffs do not snap cleanly, give them another minute — our guide on how to roast makhana covers the exact doneness test.
  • Dates for vrat. Blend the dates with a little warm milk into a paste before adding — you get even sweetness with no chunks, and no refined sugar.
  • Adding jaggery? Take the pan off the heat and let the kheer cool for a couple of minutes first, or the milk can split.
  • Chilled variation. Refrigerate for 2 to 3 hours and serve cold — the kheer sets thicker, almost like a phirni. Loosen with a splash of warm milk if needed.

Making it for Sawan or Navratri? Read this first

The base recipe is already falahari, but fasting rules vary between families, so double-check three things. First, the sweetener: sugar is accepted in most households, but if yours avoids refined sugar during vrat, use dates or mishri. Second, salt: this kheer needs none, but if you like a tiny pinch to round out the sweetness, it must be sendha namak (rock salt) — regular iodised salt is not used on fasting days. Third, the makhana itself: use plain raw phool makhana that you roast at home in ghee, not a flavoured or seasoned pack. Frequently asked questions

Can we make makhana kheer in Sawan vrat?

Haan, bilkul. Sawan vrat mein makhana phalahar mana jata hai, isliye makhana kheer ekdum vrat-friendly hai. Bas cheeni ki jagah dates ya vrat-approved mithaas use karein aur namak chahiye toh sirf sendha namak daalein. Doodh, ghee, ilaichi aur kesar sab vrat mein allowed hain, toh ye kheer Sawan somvar ke liye perfect hai.

What can I use instead of sugar in makhana kheer?

Dates are the easiest swap — blend 4 to 5 seedless dates with a little warm milk and stir the paste in at the sweetening step. Mishri (rock sugar) also works and is popular for vrat. If you prefer jaggery, take the pan off the heat and let the kheer cool for a couple of minutes before stirring it in, or the milk can split.

How long does makhana kheer keep?

Cool it fully, then store in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 2 days. It thickens as it chills because the makhana keeps absorbing milk — loosen it with a splash of warm milk before serving. Do not leave milk-based kheer at room temperature for long, especially in warm weather.

Which makhana should I use for kheer?

Use plain, unflavoured raw phool makhana so the kheer tastes of milk, cardamom and saffron rather than seasoning. Look for large, white, crisp puffs and roast them in ghee at home just before cooking. Flavoured roasted makhana like peri peri or masala is made for snacking and will clash with a sweet kheer.
